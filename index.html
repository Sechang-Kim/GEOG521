Background

Why School Space Needs a Different Lens

Existing research on children and students' everyday geographies shows that young people do not use space uniformly. Students may move differently, perceive risk differently, and attach distinct meanings to ordinary places such as classrooms, hallways, cafeterias, playgrounds, and routes to school.

This project begins from the assumption that students are not a homogeneous population. Immigrant-background students and non-immigrant students may inhabit, navigate, interpret, and feel school-related spaces in different ways. The goal is to examine the gap between officially defined school space and lived school space.

Research Questions

From Location to Meaning to Emotion

01

Everyday Spaces

Do immigrant-background students and non-immigrant students differ in the spaces they use, inhabit, or avoid?

02

Interpretation

Even when students occupy similar locations, do they notice, interpret, and describe those places differently?

03

Emotion

How are spatial practices connected to comfort, discomfort, worry, fear, happiness, belonging, or exclusion?

Methods

Geotagged Photovoice and Qualitative GIS

The project would use geotagged photovoice as a central method, asking students to document meaningful, ordinary, comforting, uncomfortable, or emotionally charged spaces related to school life. Participants could also add captions, audio notes, drawings, or short reflections to explain what they documented and why it matters.

Spatial Visualization

GIS can identify recurring locations, routes, clusters, and activity spaces where experiences concentrate, overlap, or diverge.

Qualitative Coding

Captions, stories, drawings, and reflections can be coded for recurring meanings, emotional themes, and place-based narratives.

Reflexive Mapping

Maps can use layered annotation, color coding, and place-emotion matrices without reducing lived experience to simple numbers.

Theory

Official Space and Lived Space

Drawing on Lefebvre, the project focuses on spatial practice and representational space: how students actually move through school environments and how they imagine, interpret, and feel those places. A hallway may be institutionally defined as a neutral circulation space, yet experienced as a site of surveillance, anxiety, safety, or belonging.

Critical GIS

Listen First, Map Later

A key principle is that not everything meaningful is fully mappable. The study should avoid forcing students into predefined categories or researcher-designed layers before their experiences are heard.

Participatory mapping is shaped by inclusion, exclusion, institutions, and technical design choices. This project therefore treats ambiguous, partial, contradictory, and hard-to-locate experiences as important evidence rather than noise.

Contribution

What This Project Adds

  1. It challenges the tendency to treat students as one homogeneous group.
  2. It combines photovoice, qualitative interpretation, and GIS while foregrounding lived meaning and emotion.
  3. It uses spatial analysis to explore everyday educational experience, not only administratively legible school space.
