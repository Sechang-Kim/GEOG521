Church Classification as Counter-Mapping

Important Method

The categories classify public OSM names and tags. They do not prove the race, ethnicity, or identity of congregants.

Reflection

Classification Logic and Interpretation

1. What logic guided your classification?

My classification was guided by Martin Luther King Jr.'s argument that Sunday at 11:00 AM is one of the most segregated times in American life. This observation suggests that churches should not be understood only as a single institutional category. Even when places share the same label, such as "church," their meanings can differ greatly depending on race, ethnicity, memory, and belonging. For this reason, I classified churches through visible community signals in OSM names and tags, focusing on Asian churches in one map and Black churches in the other. This differs from the planner or community view in the original lab because it does not classify facilities by function, service, or public utility. Instead, it asks how the same religious landscape may be experienced differently by different subjects.

2. What changes appeared on your map?

Under this classification, the map changed from a simple distribution of churches into a map of possible belonging, unfamiliarity, and affective experience. Some churches became more visible because their names or tags publicly signal Asian or Black religious community identity. These places were categorized as "Spiritual and Belonging." Other churches were not treated as negative or exclusionary. Because churches are religious spaces, I assume they may welcome people across ethnic boundaries. Therefore, I used the phrase "Unfamiliar, Yet Welcoming" rather than terms such as uncomfortable or unsafe. This category points to places that may not immediately feel culturally familiar, but still hold the possibility of hospitality, encounter, and social mixing. The map therefore highlights experience and affection, not only location.

3. How would you present your map?

I would present this map to church leaders, community organizations, students, and urban researchers as a conversation tool rather than a definitive demographic map. I would emphasize that the categories are based on visible OSM signals, not direct knowledge of congregant identity. The purpose is to ask how religious spaces become legible as places of belonging, and how other spaces might still become welcoming through social encounter. For design, I avoided highly oppositional color pairs such as black and white or red and blue, because the map is not meant to create a harsh binary. Instead, I used warm colors, `#DA895A` and `#E9C386`, to suggest openness, ambiguity, and the possibility of social mix.

Step 5: Classification Function 
def classify_by_patterns(row, patterns):
    text = " ".join(
        str(row.get(col, ""))
        for col in ["name", "denomination", "operator", "description"]
    ).lower()

    for pattern in patterns:
        if re.search(pattern, text, flags=re.IGNORECASE):
            return "Spiritual and Belonging"

    return "Unfamiliar, Yet Welcoming"
